# Challenging the Need for Packet Spraying in Large-Scale Distributed Training

<!-- id=238683564 parent=238780418 -->

## Overview

Large-scale distributed training in production datacenters is heavily constrained by network communication bottlenecks. The prevailing belief in both industry (e.g., Ultra Ethernet Consortium) and academia is that packet spraying is essential to improve performance. This paper challenges that assumption and asks whether single-path transport can perform as effectively as an optimal multipath transport. The authors argue that, due to key workload characteristics in distributed training (e.g., concurrent flow start times, equal flow sizes, collective communication dependencies), single-path transport with minimal flow splitting can match the performance of packet spraying in terms of maximum congestion. The paper analytically demonstrates this claim and provides preliminary evaluations to support it, proposing an alternative direction for transport protocol design tailored to distributed training.

## Objective

This paper aims to demonstrate that single-path transport with minimal flow splitting at the application layer can achieve near-optimal performance compared to multipath transport with packet spraying, particularly in the context of distributed deep learning workloads in datacenter environments.

## Background

Large-scale distributed training, Network communication bottlenecks, Packet spraying, Single-path vs. multipath transport, Deep learning workload communication patterns, Congestion control and load balancing in datacenters

## As-Is

- The dominant assumption in both academia and industry is that packet spraying is necessary to optimize network performance for distributed training.

- Distributed training workloads exhibit unique properties:

  - Flows within a collective communication pattern start almost simultaneously.

  - Flow sizes are nearly equal.

  - Completion time of a collective operation is more critical than individual flow completion times.

  - Flows can be dynamically partitioned upon arrival based on network conditions and workload characteristics.

- Traditional congestion control mechanisms in datacenters are designed for generic workloads and do not fully exploit the structured communication patterns of distributed training.

- Packet spraying is widely used in multipath transport to distribute network load, but it introduces overhead due to increased packet reordering, out-of-order delivery, and excessive buffer usage, as well as complexity in ensuring fair load balancing and maintaining consistent flow completion times.

- The inefficiency in existing solutions stems from treating deep learning training communication as generic traffic rather than leveraging its structured nature.

## To-Be

- The authors propose an approach where single-path transport is enhanced with minimal flow splitting at the application layer to achieve comparable performance to packet spraying.

- They analytically demonstrate that this approach maintains network congestion levels comparable to ideal multipath transport by leveraging workload-specific properties such as synchronized flow initiation and controlled splitting at the application layer.

- The methodology relies on key properties of distributed training workloads:

  - Simultaneous flow start times enable efficient congestion management.

  - Equal-sized flows allow predictable load balancing.

  - Prioritizing collective completion times over individual flow completion allows optimizations such as adaptive congestion control, workload-aware scheduling, and efficient bandwidth allocation tailored to distributed training patterns.

  - Controlled flow splitting can mitigate congestion without the complexity of full packet spraying.

- The paper suggests revisiting the design of transport protocols to tailor them specifically to the needs of large-scale distributed training rather than applying generic solutions.

## Methodology

- Analytical modeling of network congestion under different transport mechanisms, considering factors such as flow synchronization, congestion hotspots, queuing delays, and load balancing efficiency.

- Evaluation using real-world distributed training workloads, including deep learning models such as GPT, BERT, and ResNet trained on large-scale datasets like ImageNet and OpenWebText.

- Performance comparison between single-path transport with minimal flow splitting and multipath transport with packet spraying.

- Simulated datacenter network environments with deep learning workload characteristics.

- Measurement of congestion levels, flow completion times, and overall training efficiency.

## Results

- The proposed single-path transport with minimal flow splitting achieves performance comparable to packet spraying in terms of congestion control.

- Preliminary results suggest that specialized transport protocols for distributed training can simplify network management while maintaining efficiency.

- Future research should explore the full implementation of such protocols and their integration into existing distributed training frameworks.

- Potential optimizations in hardware and software to further enhance transport mechanisms for deep learning workloads.

- Broader implications for designing network architectures that specifically cater to AI training demands rather than general datacenter traffic.
